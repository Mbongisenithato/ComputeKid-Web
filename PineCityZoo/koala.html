Overview

The koala (*Phascolarctos cinereus*), sometimes inaccurately called "koala bear," is an **arboreal herbivorous marsupial** native to Australia. It is the **only extant representative of the family Phascolarctidae**, and its closest living relatives are **wombats**. Koalas are found in **coastal areas of the mainland‚Äôs eastern and southern regions**, inhabiting **Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, and South Australia**.

Physical Characteristics

Koalas are **easily recognizable** by their **stout, tailless body**, **large head**, **round, fluffy ears**, and **large, spoon-shaped nose**. Their body length ranges **from 60 to 85 cm (24 to 33 in)**, and their weight varies **from 4 to 15 kg (9 to 33 lb)**. Their **pelage color** ranges from **silver-grey to chocolate brown**. Northern populations of koalas tend to be **smaller and lighter in color** than those further south.

Habitat & Behavior

Koalas primarily **inhabit open eucalypt woodlands**, and their **diet consists mostly of eucalyptus leaves**. Since eucalyptus has **limited nutritional value**, koalas are **largely sedentary** and can **sleep up to 20 hours a day**.

Koalas are **asocial animals**, with **bonding occurring only between mothers and their offspring**. Adult males **communicate with loud bellows**, used to **intimidate rivals and attract mates**. They **mark their presence** using **secretions from scent glands located on their chests**.

Reproduction & Threats

Being **marsupials**, koalas give birth to **underdeveloped young** that **crawl into their mother‚Äôs pouch**, staying there **for the first 6‚Äì7 months of their lives**. These young, called **joeys**, are fully weaned **around one year old**.

Koalas have **few natural predators**, but they are **threatened by diseases**, including **Chlamydiaceae bacteria** and the **koala retrovirus**, as well as **bushfires and droughts**.
